Overview

An underworld steeped in myth.

Curse of Osiris is a personal prototype set in ancient Egypt — a roguelite dungeon crawler built in Unity and heavily inspired by Supergiant's Hades. The core premise: a soul condemned to the Egyptian underworld must fight through procedurally-arranged rooms, slowly uncovering the betrayal that led to their death.

Rather than simply mimicking Hades aesthetically, the design goal was to study what makes that game's loop so satisfying — then transplant those principles into a completely different mythology, tone, and set of mechanics.

"The mythology isn't decoration — it's the rulebook. Every mechanic had to feel like it belonged to this world."
Design Goals

What I set out to solve.

  • Create a narrative-driven combat loop where story rewards are tied to mechanical progression
  • Design room layouts with natural chokepoints and looping escape routes
  • Build a boon system grounded in Egyptian deities (Ra, Anubis, Thoth) with distinct playstyle identities
  • Ensure the death-and-rebirth loop feels intentional — not punishing, but purposeful
  • Script ambient dialogue that reacts to player decisions without breaking pace
Process

From concept to prototype.

Research & Reference
Deep analysis of Hades' loop — beat maps, room density, dialogue triggers, and boon probability curves. Mapped Egyptian mythology's structure to find equivalent narrative beats.
GDD Draft
Wrote a detailed Game Design Document covering the core loop, room archetypes (combat, elite, rest, shop, boss), currency systems, and narrative delivery method.
Level Blocking
Built blockout levels in Unity using modular tile sets. Playtested for movement feel, sightlines, and spawn-point fairness before adding any visual dressing.
Narrative Layer
Scripted reactive dialogue for the guide character (based on Osiris) using a simple state machine — different lines triggered by low health, first-time room encounters, and run count milestones.
Playtest & Iteration
Three rounds of internal playtesting focused on pacing, difficulty curve, and whether players understood the boon system without explicit tutorialisation.
